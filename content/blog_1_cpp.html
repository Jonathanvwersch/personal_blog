While most companies have struggled to deal with the COVID-19 pandemic, organisations offering massive open online courses have thrived as more and more people are turning to online education. One such company is Udacity. Started in 2011 by German computer scientist Sebastian Thrun, Udacity is perhaps best known for its Nanodegree program — three to four-month-long courses on a range of topics related to business and programming.

\r\n

While these courses usually cost upwards of $300 per month (and are expected to take 3–4 months at 10 hours a week), from the period of March to July, Udacity was offering one free month of access to any of their Nanodegrees. After finishing university, and finding all my graduation travel plans cancelled, I thought I would use my time wisely and attempt to finish Udacity’s C++ Nanodegree within the one-month free trial. After doing so back in June, here are my thoughts on the course.

\r\n

Course Structure

\r\n

The course is made up of five projects, each of which is preceded by short informational videos, as well as exercises.

\r\n

Project 1: Building a route planner to find the shortest path between two points on a map

\r\n

Project 2: Building an object-oriented Linux system monitor

\r\n

Project 3: Creating a chat bot, with a focus on memory management, which answers questions related to C++

\r\n

Project 4: Development of a traffic light simulator using multi-threading principles

\r\n

Project 5: Build your own C++ application from scratch or develop on top of an already built Snake game

\r\n

You can choose to program the projects either on your computer or on Udacity’s virtual Linux machine. While each project has specific criteria which must be met to pass the course, you are free to go the extra mile and add additional features outside the scope of the assignment.

\r\n

The Good

\r\n

1) The feedback you receive for each project submission is excellent. It is personalised, detailed, and constructive.

\r\n

2) You are given a lot of free reign in the final project. This means you can build just about anything you want so long as it meets the criteria, which are not limiting by any means.

\r\n

3) You are provided with a range of videos on improving your online presence with regards to LinkedIn and Github, which are both useful and insightful. And you have the opportunity to have your LinkedIn profile reviewed and critiqued by a member of the Udacity team.

\r\n

4) The course covers a range of topics, from the more basic elements of C++ such as classes and loops, to more complex subjects like move semantics, and dynamic memory allocation.

\r\n

5) Udacity’s Student Hub is a great place to chat and ask for help from other students undertaking the Nanodegree.

\r\n

6) The first two projects are excellent and give you a good insight into the fundamentals of C++, as well as object-oriented programming.

\r\n

The Bad

\r\n

1) It is very expensive. If you pay upfront, it will cost you over $1000.

\r\n

2) The learning material leading up to projects three and four is poorly taught. Memory management is so critical in C++ and I would have loved if the teaching quality had been better for that section of the course. I also did not enjoy projects three and four. I didn’t find them particularly difficult but I also did not understand what I was doing, which meant that I completed the projects having learned very little.

\r\n

3) There are occasional UI issues, particularly in the latter part of the course. For some reason, Udacity chose to cram a lot of the learning material into small boxes, which had scaling issues leading to a lot of text being cut off. Evident by messages left in the Student Hub, this was not an issue I faced alone.

\r\n

4) Each Nanodegree has a forum where you can ask questions to mentors when you are stuck on a certain aspect of a project. For the most part, the answers from the mentors are quite poor.

\r\n

Verdict

\r\n

Don’t bother taking this course. I mean I don’t regret it cause I did it for free but in no way could I recommend this course to someone who would be paying full price. The project feedback is great but the teaching, particularly in the latter stages of the course, is too poor to justify the steep price. It is important to note that you are expected to have a basic knowledge of C++ before taking the course and I think that that is a fair statement. Ultimately, while Udacity’s C++ teachers are well qualified in terms of their programming abilities, great programmers do not necessarily make great teachers and I think that this course is proof of that.

\r\n

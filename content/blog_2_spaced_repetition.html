How to Remember Anything: Active Recall, Spaced Repetition and the Note Taking Fallacy

It’s a familiar interaction we all know too well — you meet someone for the first time, you exchange handshakes, niceties, and then five minutes later you realise that you’ve completely forgotten their name. How is that possible? Is our memory just that frail or are there other forces at work? The question of why we tend to forget information so quickly is far too complex to be answered in this article. But providing an answer to how we can improve our memory retention is possible. To do so, let’s begin our story in 19th century Germany.

Hermann Ebbinghaus and The Forgetting Curve

In 1885, German scientist Hermann Ebbinghaus published his seminal work, *Memory: A Contribution to Experimental Psychology*. The monograph described various experiments he conducted on himself related to learning and forgetting. The most ground-breaking of which involved memorising a list of logatomes or nonsensical syllables — consonant-vowel-consonants where the consonant does not repeat and the syllable has no prior meaning (examples of which include dax, mek, gim, hes). These nonsensical syllables, 2300 in total, were chosen as they have no meaning, and therefore eliminate any confounding factors. Ebbinghaus spent more than a year meticulously recording how many times he studied each list, the time interval between his study sessions, as well as how much he was able to remember. Then, to prove that these results were not accidental, he repeated the same experiment one year later.

Portrait of Hermann Ebbinghaus

While his experiments were internally valid — that is to say, he avoided most confounding factors — they lacked external validity as he was the sole subject in his study. Nonetheless, the results of his work served as one of the first instances of an individual attempting to record how memory decays over time and culminated in what would later go on to be named the Forgetting Curve.

Plot is adapted from the tabulated data presented on pages 67–74 of Ebbinghaus’ monograph

The Forgetting Curve pictured above was plotted using data collected from Ebbinghaus’ experiments. The results are frankly quite astonishing. Within one day, he had forgotten over 70% of what he had learned. Perhaps with findings such as these, it is unsurprising that we often forget peoples’ names so quickly.

The issue with Ebbinghaus’ interpretation of the Forgetting Curve is that it suggests that all memories simply fade away over time, which is not entirely true. After all, why is it that we can remember old phone numbers and addresses, which we haven’t thought about for many years?

Robert Bjork’s Theory of Forgetting

Robert Bjork is a Distinguished Professor of Psychology at the University of California, Los Angeles. He has dedicated the bulk of his career to better understanding human learning and memory.

Bjork’s theory of forgetting%20something%20is.) states that memories can be distinguished by two different strengths, namely retrieval strength and storage strength.

1) Retrieval strength refers to how likely you are to access and recall a memory right now. For instance, to score highly on an exam, the memory in question needs to exhibit high retrieval strength.

2) Storage strength refers to how deeply embedded a certain memory is in your mind. The idea is that barring some form of brain damage, storage strength, unlike retrieval strength, cannot decrease over time but can increase through repeated recall.

Examples of memories with varying retrieval and storage strengths

Therefore, Ebbinghaus’ Forgetting Curve is not a depiction of how our memories fade away over time but rather a graphical representation of the decay in our retrieval strength. The memories are there, we just need to bring them back to the surface. Enter in spaced repetition.

Spaced repetition is a learning technique that involves reviewing previously learned material at systematic intervals. By doing so, one can retain information for long periods of time. Ebbinghaus was aware of the effectiveness of spaced repetition. In his research, he concluded that

“With any considerable number of repetitions, a suitable distribution of them over a space of time is decidedly more advantageous than the massing of them at a single time.”

But despite these findings, Ebbinghaus did not study the effect of spaced repetition on memory retrievability. In fact, in-depth research on the effectiveness of spaced repetition would only take place nearly a century later courtesy of a Polish researcher who sought to find the optimal study interval times to maximise memory retention.

Piotr Wozniak, Active Recall and the SuperMemo Algorithm

In the 1980s Polish researcher Piotr Wozniak) grew frustrated with the sheer amount of information he had to learn while at university. His frustrations led him to develop SuperMemo, the first software to take advantage of spaced repetition. The software enables users to practice active recall (learning by testing) by allowing them to save questions and their respective answers. The software’s ingenuity is that it would show saved questions to the user when the algorithm believes that they are about to forget or lose their retrieval strength of the memory. The user then must rate their relative ease of recall (from 1 to 5). This allows the algorithm to “learn” when next to ask the question to the user.

One of the few pictures that exist online of the elusive Wozniak (courtesy of a brilliant Wired article on his life and work), who has preferred to live a life of anonymity.

Piotr’s research and software brought rise to an updated version of the Forgetting Curve, which provides a more holistic representation of how our memories decay over time. The idea being that we can flatten the curve and reset decay by recalling memories at systematic intervals.

Wozniak’s Forgetting Curve enforced that idea that we can improve a memory’s retrieval strength through repeated recall at specific intervals — the length of which he optimised using his SuperMemo algorithm.

In the decades since, several studies have verified the notion that active recall coupled with spaced repetition is the most effective form of learning. I invite you to read this study from 2010 or this one from 2011 for proof of these claims.

The unfortunate reality is that very few people are aware of the effectiveness of pairing these two study methods. Students favour note taking and summarising, despite their relative ineffectiveness. In a 2013 paper, ten different techniques were evaluated for their effectiveness in improving learning. What they found was that summarisation, highlighting, and rereading were all wholly ineffective methods of learning while practice testing (active recall) and distributed practice (active recall coupled with spaced repetition) were the most optimum methods.

In a 2013 study, ten different study techniques were tested for their ability to improve one’s learning capabilities, revealing spaced repetition (distributed practice) and active recall (practice testing) to be the most effective.

So, what does this all mean? Should you stop taking notes? Should you adopt spaced repetition and active recall into your learning? According to the research — yes. But the reality is, of course, a bit more complicated. If you fair well as a note-taker, then maybe there is no reason to stop, but for those who are struggling to memorise information, it’s probably time to listen to the experts.

Incorporating Active Recall and Spaced Repetition into your Learning

The best way to incorporate active recall into your learning is to start asking more questions. Rather than rereading or summarising your notes, create questions out of them. These could be in the form of flashcards or quizzes. The next important step is to optimally space out your study intervals.

If you prefer to create your flashcards manually, you can determine your spaced repetition intervals using methods such as the Leitner System. For digital flashcard learning, I’d recommend Anki — a web and mobile application that allows you to create flashcards and study them using spaced repetition.
